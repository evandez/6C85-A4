Proposition: Climate change is increasing the rate of natural disasters across the world.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (-.5) Subset of data:I focused on flood natural disasters over other natural disasters because I noticed during my exploratory analysis that the most profound change in natural disasters over time has been with floods: they've become significantly more frequent in the past twenty years, especially in heavily coastal countries. I felt this was a tad deceptive as other disasters have not increased in frequency quite as much, though they have increased. I opted to include the sea level indicators alongside the natural disaster indicators to tie the figure back to global warming; I felt that readers more strongly associated sea levels with global warming.
  • (+1) Shape: I chose to use an area plot for an affective appeal to "rising waters." I wanted readers to feel a sense of urgency when looking at both the increase in sea level and flood disasters. Initially, I considered using a quieter visual representation (e.g. bars) for the flood counts, but decided against it because it added visual noise and drew too much attention to specific years, when I wanted readers to focus most on the overall trend of "rising" values.
  • (-1) Selecting the axis ranges: I intentionally restricted the y axis for sea level to positive values, hiding the fact that sea levels decreased prior to the mid 1990s and the fact that floods were becoming more common even prior to sea levels starting to rise. I wanted readers to not get distracted by the negative values and to focus mostly on the affective experience of rising waters.
  • (1.5) Data transformations: I chose to keep each measurement in terms of absolute counts rather than reporting fractions or percentages. The main data transformations I performed were averaging the sea level changes for each year, normalizing out the different measuring equipment used by NOAA and the different seas, and joining the sea level measurements with the flood disaster counts (summed over countries).

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (-2) Data selection: Perhaps the most deceptive thing I did in this plot was to join the natural disaster count indicators with the surface temperature indicators, and use this to filter the data down in a seemingly principled way. Specifically, I noticed two trends in the data before making the plot: (1) floods made up most of the increase in natural disasters, and (2) the countries that had the greatest increases in temperatures also had the smallest increase in floods over the years. Based on this observation, I decided to focus the plot on the relationship between surface temperature (literal "global warming") with natural disaster frequency, and could thus justify filtering the dataset to only the 50 countries experiencing the most warming. I included all natural disasters in the count to help smooth out the bars, resulting bars for natural disasters look quite flat, even when you ignore the small axis ranges I describe below.
  • (1) Data transformation: All of the values plotted in this figure are five-year moving averages, intended to denoise the plot a bit. I initially tried to make my visualizations without this transformation, but they were too noisy to interpret, even though the same trends appeared. I communciated this openly in labels of the plot.
  • (-1) Axis ranges: I shrunk the natural disaster axis range primarily to make the red line more visible, but also to make the natural disaster bars look smaller and more benign. Part of the reason I do not label this more deceptive is that I want to call the most attention to the rising temperatures, while the bars themselves are (intentionally) flat even scaled up.
  • (.5) Labeling, colors, etc.: I made an effort for this figure to feel scientific and legitimate, labeling each axis with an exact description of the value and with the units in parentheses. I chose a deep, warm color for the temperature line to nod to "warming" (and to make it feel like a legitimate issue!), while also choosing a soft green color for the bars to suggest "nothing to see here."

Final Reflection

Looking back at these two designs, by far the hardest part was identifying what story to tell. When it comes to climate data, things often look bleak; it's very hard not to show increasing trends in metrics like natural disaster counts or temperature. I ultimately found myself performing a lot of exploratory data analysis in this project, trying to tease apart the relationships between different climate indicators and the other variables (country, year, etc.) in the dataset. Once I had identified the relationship between floods the total natural disaster counts, however, it became a lot easier to reason about the data, and the remaining aspects of my designs fell into place. From there, it was straightforward to decide what to filter, what metrics to average versus show counts for, what colors to use, and so on, because I had discovered the underlying pattern I wanted to use and the I knew how I wanted the reader to feel when looking at the visualizations. I leaned in as heavily as I could to the affective aspect of the design, while preserving some amount of scientific integrity by using fairly standard methods of data aggregation and fairly honest axis labels.

At multiple points during the design process, I found myself looking for data transformations that helped to tell my story. Once particular instance of this was using the five-year moving averages in the "against" figure: though it does not dramatically change the conclusion of the figure, it makes the conclusion feel more "absolute." Connecting this back to the idea of ethical data analysis, some part of performing these transformations feels wrong, because you as the designer are, to some extent, fishing for a story where there might not be one. On the other hand, visualizations are designed to communicate, and I think there's a fair framing of these kinds of data transformations as "denoising" to better present the signal. I think the key boundaries depend on how transparent you are with yourself and with your audience. It's okay and necessary to augment data, to exclude data, and to mold it in order to tell a better story, so long as you're aware of what you're doing and honest about it. I think it becomes distinctly unethical when you see the true trend and try to hide it.

To conclude, I've left this project feeling that the boundary between persuasive and deceptive techniques is extremely narrow, and that it depends highly on the mission. One must ask, as a designer: Am I setting out to discover the truth and persuade people of it, or am I setting out to persuade people of my beliefs? And consequently, one must also ask as a reader: what is the designer's objective in showing me this visualization--to bring me to their worldview or to work with me to discover the truth?
